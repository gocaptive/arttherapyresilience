Connecting Community After Tragedy

How do we help ourselves heal, and others recover?
Our goal is to use art to reach communities traumatized after natural or man-made disasters.

Our goal is to:

- Promote healing
- Increase communication with others regarding traumatic events
- Gain an understanding of community outreach and benefits through art
- Create a template for connecting communities after tragedy

QUICK STATS

In 2018, there have been over 105 mass shootings (4+ victims injured or killed excluding the subject/suspect/perpetrator at one location) along the East Coast. A well-known incident that received significant news coverage took place on February 14, 2018, when a gunman attacked and killed 17 students and staff, and leaving 14 additional victims injured at Marjory Stoneman Douglas High School in Parkland, Florida (Gun Violence Archive, 2018). Communities experience unspeakable trauma. Once the media coverage lapses, the real work begins as they struggle to rebuild and risk being forgotten by the rest of the world. 

AFTERMATH

While mass shootings make headlines, the attention weans over time. Sandy Phillips who lost her 24-year-old daughter Jessi said, “When you lose a child violently and publicly, there’s an outpouring of support at first.” But that attention fades. “Once the vigils are over and the media is gone, that’s when things get really bad. The world moves on, and you don’t. You can’t. It’s a pain you can’t outrun” (Edwards & Luscombe, November, 2018). 

UNITING COMMUNITY THROUGH ART

Connecting Communities of Resilience Through Art Therapy aims to unite communities through art and healing. According to DiSunno, Linton, & Bowes (2011), traumatic events can be processed and visualized in a secure manner through the creation of artwork. Often, when clients are asked to verbally describe a traumatic experience, there is a risk of re-traumatization. Through art, trauma can be re-integrated into enduring memories in a sustained way. This facilitates memory reorganization and healing, "helping the child to learn how to negotiate these difficult intense feelings through pictorial description can, in turn, be helpful in encouraging their creation of a narrative about their experiences" (Disunno, Linton, & Bowes, 2011). Connecting Communities of Resilience Through Art Therapy works with at-risk-children (those who reside in high-risk towns, or those who are on the periphery).

FUNDING AND SESSIONS

There will be no cost to participants. Adjunct assistance will be provided by a Licensed Creative Arts Therapist and Creative Arts Therapy Graduate Students from Hofstra University as well as other volunteers. The duration is a one or two day session.

The artifact from this project (the art quilts) will be used to generate public interest and bring attention to this programming and encourage funding for future projects. Any art material donation for use during the session is welcome! The experience will be recorded with the hopes of submission for publication and presentation at the 2019 American Art Therapy Conference so other communities may learn from this significant pilot project. 

A community art exchange is a place to start. 

Students will create individual imagery on 6” x 6” adhesive vinyl sheets using various media that will then be adhered to mural material and delivered to Parkland, Florida. We believe there is power in numbers and in being remembered. It’s an uplifting, positive, safe way to connect. Natural and human-made disasters create a profound impact; they must know they are not alone. It is an opportunity to generate hope and come from a place of resilience.
